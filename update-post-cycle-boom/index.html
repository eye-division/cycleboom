Update post cycle BOOM

February 13, 2017 By Tim Jones Leave a Comment Filed Under: Uncategorized

Pin It

Welcome back to the cycle BOOM blog. After a busy crescendo to the project, with our conferences in London and Manchester to launch our findings and recommendations in September 2016, we bring you up to date with recent developments and future plans.

The final conferences were successful in bringing together a total of more than 80 people with in interest in older people’s cycling. In London these included Brian Deegan, Principal Technical Specialist with responsibility for cycling at Transport for London and other TfL staff along with older cycling enthusiasts and academics from as far away as Australia. We were able to set out and discuss the findings from the many approaches we had used to provide a rounded understanding of older people’s cycling. These included life history interviews to ascertain the patterns in people’s experiences of cycling (and when they did not); detailed observations and discussions of rides through Oxford, Bristol, Reading and Cardiff and wellbeing trials to record the effects of returning to pedal biking or trying electrically assisted cycling for eight weeks. In Manchester we had partnered with the Age Friendly Manchester team to ensure our recommendations in our Summary Report were usefully related to the World Health Organisation’s Age-Friendly domains set out in the Global Age-friendly Cities guide (WHO 2007). This partnership also resulted in a room full-to-bursting at Manchester Central Library with cycling advocates, environmental campaigners, local government policy officers and designers.

One of the key messages from our Summary Report was the need for a ‘substantial shift in approach and policy across different sectors and partnership working at all levels’. In order to help achieve this we produced a series of Briefing Notes for Planners, Engineers & Designers, Health Promoters and the Cycle Industry.  We have also been continuing to spread the word about our research to a wide range of stakeholders with an influence on how cycling can be enabled. These have included MPs, the Public Health Network Cymru, Oxfordshire County Council Public Health, Cycling and Transport officers, the NHS Healthy New Towns Research and Innovation Summit, Bristol Ageing Better, the Adaptation and Resilience in the Context of Change network (ARCC), the Age Action Alliance Transport Working Group, Raleigh UK designers and marketers, Employers Health and Planning Events, the London E-bike Forum and academic conferences on mobility and transport in Edinburgh and Washington DC. Responses have been very positive with an increased understanding of the potential multiple benefits to health and wellbeing that cycling can bring and often surprise at how much electrically assisted e-biking has to offer. We have also had constructive conversations about how best to overcome the many barriers to enabling safe, convenient and enjoyable everyday cycling for people of all ages.

Our future plans include a range of initiatives. The final cycle BOOM videos explaining the methods and findings from our life history interviews and wellbeing trials are in the edit suite and will be appearing on our website soon. Look out for news on our Twitter feed. We are working with the Broken Spoke co-operative to establish a programme of social rides for older people in Oxford. The Pedalling On programme will offer short guided rides to local residents and links to the wider services such as help with fixing bikes also available from Broken Spoke. We are also presenting our findings to Sustrans and the Oxford and Manchester Cycle Forums. Plans for further work with Oxfordshire County Council, Manchester City Council, the International Longevity Centre UK and BikePlus will examine how healthy urban mobility for all ages can best be delivered through Age Friendly Cities designed for cycling and walking.

 

We would love to hear from anyone who would like to work with us on these issues or who would like us to speak at their event. Please email Tim Jones, the Principal Investigator tjones@brookes.ac.uk.
